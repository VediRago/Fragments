# Three Rules

## Purpose

This file defines a simple method for building systems, worlds, and narrative ideas in Fragments.

The method has three parts:

1. Treasure Map
2. Key
3. Treasure

The goal is to make sure every important idea has structure, discovery, and emotional payoff.

---

## 1. Treasure Map

The treasure map is the visible trail.

It is what the reader or player can notice before they understand the full meaning.

A treasure map can be:

- a repeated image
- a strange rule
- a cultural detail
- a name
- a behavior
- a contradiction
- a system that feels unusual
- a piece of history that seems decorative at first

The treasure map should not explain itself immediately.

It should create curiosity and recognition.

The audience should feel that something matters before they know why.

Example:

A statue of an emperor in Sera's prose can first feel like world texture. Later, it can become evidence of the historical figure who made Erit's world possible.

---

## 2. Key

The key is the information, experience, or realization that lets the audience reinterpret the treasure map.

The key should not feel like a random explanation added later.

It should unlock what was already there.

A key can be:

- a later scene
- a character wound
- a historical truth
- a system rule
- a repeated behavior in a new context
- a missing piece of information
- a shift in perspective

The key changes the meaning of the map without erasing the first reading.

The reader should feel:

> I was right to notice this, but I did not know what it meant yet.

Example:

The historical founder of Erit's world can first appear as a divine or imperial figure. The key is learning that he was a displaced child raised by Rome, shaped by family, language, and legacy, and wounded by wanting a kind of family his origin could not give him.

---

## 3. Treasure

The treasure is the emotional or structural payoff.

It is the moment where the audience can look back and understand why the detail existed.

The treasure should make the story, world, or system feel less random and more inevitable.

It should create the feeling:

> I get it now. It came from somewhere.

The treasure is not only cleverness.

It has to change how the audience feels about what they already saw.

Example:

A civilization obsessed with inheritance, dynasties, preservation, and continuity becomes more meaningful when its foundation can be traced back to a displaced child who learned to desire family and legacy after being taken into another world.

---

## Rule of Use

Do not add a treasure map unless there is a key.

Do not add a key unless it leads to treasure.

Do not add treasure that only proves the story is clever.

The strongest treasure changes feeling.

---

## Design Value

This method helps prevent weak bandaids.

Instead of solving problems by adding sudden explanations, it builds ideas with roots and payoff.

It also supports game design.

A quest, dialogue branch, or world detail becomes stronger when it has:

- something the player can notice
- something that changes the meaning of what they noticed
- something worth realizing at the end

This is how systems become emotional instead of only functional.
