# Ilunis Ventari — Founding Era

## Core Identity

Ilunis is the second son of House Ventari.

He is overused because he is strong.

Rutare is overprotected because he is heir.

Ilunis is overused because he is useful.

The family sends him into danger because he can survive it, until one day he cannot survive it whole.

## Core Experience

Ilunis is maimed at the Battle of Augunar while defending Ventari lands from invaders.

He falls from his horse and is crushed in the battle commotion.

His lower body is permanently damaged.

After Augunar, he can barely walk.

The injury matters because Ilunis was valued for strength, movement, and usefulness in conflict.

When his body is damaged, he does not only lose mobility.

He loses the proof of worth the family had trained him to use.

## Core Wound

Ilunis feels that he threw his life away for House Ventari and still lost his place beside his father.

Viriatus becomes the living proof that sacrifice did not earn him what he wanted.

Ilunis does not only hate Viriatus.

He hates what Ecalus's love for Viriatus proves about his own sacrifice.

To Ilunis, the wound is:

> I gave my body to this family, and Father gave his pride to someone else.

His wound is not only ambition.

It is the belief that being used should entitle him to authority.

If the family spent his strength, then the family owes him place, recognition, and control.

Viriatus interrupts that exchange.

## Why Mercy And Softness Become Enemy Ideas

Ilunis learns the wrong lesson from his injury.

He does not think:

> I was used too hard by my family.

That thought would force him to blame the house he wants to belong to.

Instead, he turns the pain outward.

He decides that mercy, softness, and hesitation are what failed him.

This protects his loyalty to House Ventari while giving him something to hate.

If softness failed him, then hardness becomes wisdom.

If pain was the price he paid, then pain can become the price others should pay too.

## Inner Logic

> Mercy did not save my body.  
> Softness did not protect the lands.  
> Pain is the cost of order.  
> If something threatens the bloodline, it must be cut out.

This logic helps Ilunis survive humiliation.

If pain is necessary, then his suffering was not meaningless.

If force is order, then his damaged body can still belong to strength.

## Behavior

After Augunar, Ilunis becomes ruthless.

His humiliation turns into control because control is the closest replacement for the physical power he lost.

His later logic becomes:

- law as restored control
- punishment as proof of strength
- force as cure for humiliation
- weakness as danger
- betrayal as something that must be cut out

This is why Ilunis becomes suited to sanctioned violence later.

He needs force to mean more than cruelty.

He needs force to mean order, because then his own brutality can feel like duty.

## Ermerus Influence

Ermerus gives Ilunis's jealousy the shape of duty.

The key idea is not simply:

> I hate Viriatus.

It becomes:

> Viriatus threatens our blood.

That interpretation works because it gives Ilunis a cleaner reason than envy.

Ilunis can admit duty more easily than jealousy.

He can tell himself he is protecting the family instead of admitting he is wounded by Father's affection for Viriatus.

Ermerus presses the exact place where Ilunis is weakest:

- Ilunis gave his body for House Ventari
- Ecalus looks at Viriatus like the son he wished he had
- Viriatus is not their blood
- therefore Ilunis can believe his pain has been disrespected by an outsider inside the family

Ilunis does not need to understand the whole future.

He only needs to believe the killing is justified.

## Murder Of Viriatus

Ilunis kills Viriatus privately after Ecalus dies.

The family is mourning Ecalus.

Teresta is away.

Rutare cries or panics.

Viriatus is vulnerable, kneeling over Ecalus's casket as a grieving son.

Ilunis stabs him in the back.

The murder is not battlefield glory.

It is a private betrayal committed against someone grieving the same father.

That matters because Ilunis's later line may punish betrayal while descending from betrayal.

## What The Murder Gives Him

Killing Viriatus gives Ilunis power again.

More precisely, it restores agency after maiming.

After Augunar, Ilunis's body no longer obeys him fully.

Killing Viriatus becomes the first moment where he feels decisive, strong, and necessary again.

To Ilunis, killing Viriatus can feel like reclaiming what he is owed.

It says:

> I still decide what happens to this family.

To House Ventari, it is removal.

To Ermerus, it is material that can become public meaning.

To later Regulatus, it becomes sacrifice.

His personal wound becomes the hand of historical theft.

## Later Consequence

The Ilunis line can become tied to law, punishment, judgment, military authority, and sanctioned violence.

This is not random institutional assignment.

It grows from Ilunis's need to make force feel righteous.

His descendants turn betrayal into order because their origin must be made survivable.

They justify force as protection, judgment as stability, and punishment as duty.

They may become the family most associated with judges, military generals, and the forces that fight rebels.

Their power is loud, visible, and feared, but unstable from the inside because it is built on a contradiction.

The contradiction:

> The Ilunis line punishes betrayal while descending from betrayal.

## Modern System Direction

Ilunis's descendants may eventually control:

- courts
- judges
- military command
- rebel suppression
- public punishment
- emergency authority
- state violence justified as protection
- legal legitimacy for dynastic decisions

## Key Question

How does Ilunis turn being used into a doctrine of necessary sacrifice, authority, and punishment?
