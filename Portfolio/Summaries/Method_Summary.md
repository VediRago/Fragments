# Method Summary

This is the short version of the method I am testing in the Erit worldbuilding branch.

I began with a problem I kept noticing in games: quests, NPCs, and places can feel disconnected when they do not react to what is happening around them.

I wanted to see if worldbuilding could start from cause and consequence, instead of treating lore, factions, NPCs, and quests as separate pieces.

## Core Chain

The core chain I used while building Viriatus, House Ventari, and the early family foundation was:

> experience -> emotion -> reason -> behavior

Experience is what a person, group, city, or culture has lived through.

Emotion is the state created by that experience.

Reason is how that emotion makes sense when it meets a situation.

Behavior is what follows from that reasoning.

The important correction is that emotion does not directly force behavior. The same emotion can lead to different behavior depending on the experience behind it and the situation it meets.

## Atmosphere

Later, while testing the world and factions, I noticed that atmosphere did not need to be forced at the start.

When behavior repeated across a place, group, or institution, atmosphere started to appear by itself.

That extends the chain into:

> experience -> emotion -> reason -> behavior -> atmosphere

## Layers

Pressure alone was not enough.

The same situation can affect different people, cities, or factions in different ways because they carry different experiences and have different amounts of influence.

That led to the layer idea.

A layer is not just a label or a folder. It is a scale where experience, influence, and change act.

In the current Twine prototype, the test uses:

- Layer 1 — City / World State
- Layer 2 — Faction Pressure
- Layer 3 — NPC / Local Behavior

Those labels belong to this first small test. The useful idea is that layers can represent different scales of experience and influence depending on what is being tested.

Layers helped explain why different NPCs, districts, factions, or cities could react differently without making their behavior feel random.

## Locked And Fluid Layers

Not every layer should change at the same speed.

Some layers are fluid, such as districts, local moods, gangs, crowds, and undecided citizens.

Some layers are more stable, such as major factions, institutions, doctrine, and important characters with a fixed role.

Player choice should not make a faction suddenly stop being itself.

That does not mean a faction could never change over a long period. It means that, for a small prototype, some things need to remain stable so the test stays readable.

## NPCs

For NPCs, the method helped me build characters from the world instead of starting only with personality traits.

An NPC can be shaped by city conditions, faction pressure, local behavior, and personal history.

The NPC does not change because a dialogue option says so.

They change only when the experience gives them a new reason to behave differently.

## Quests

For game-facing material, the chain can extend into:

> experience -> emotion -> reason -> behavior -> atmosphere -> quest logic

A quest should not begin as a random task.

It should come from pressure, behavior, and atmosphere that already exist in the world.

The outcome may reinforce, weaken, or shift a layer, but only after the quest creates an experience strong enough to justify it.

## Current Finding

Behavior and atmosphere often do not need to be added separately.

When experience, emotion, reason, and layer pressure are clear, behavior becomes easier to find.

When behavior repeats, atmosphere starts to appear.

Quests can then be found inside that atmosphere instead of being placed on top of it.

This is still early and needs more testing, but it is the clearest working method I have in the project right now.
