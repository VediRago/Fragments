# Erit Worldbuilding Summary

Short version of the Erit worldbuilding case study.

The worldbuilding did not start with a full setting already planned.

It started with a practical problem:

> How do I make a calendar for Erit's world so I can place the eras in a timeline?

That question led back to a detail already inside **Fragments**: the emperor's statue.

Instead of adding a calendar from outside the story, I used that statue as the seed.

The next question was:

> Who was important enough to become the statue, and important enough for time to be counted around him?

That is how Viriatus began.

He became the figure that let the calendar, family history, public memory, and later systems of control grow from one detail.

## What Came From That

The calendar question became:

- Viriatus
- House Ventari
- the AVD calendar
- the public story of Regulatus
- the hidden truth behind Viriatus's death
- family wounds that later became institutions
- world pressure inherited by Erit's era

The worldbuilding did not grow by adding random lore.

It grew by asking what pressure created each detail, who used it, who was hurt by it, and what behavior came from it.

## Main Example

Viriatus's death does not only matter because he dies.

It matters because Ermerus turns that death into public meaning.

AVD does not begin when Viriatus privately dies.

AVD begins when Ermerus publicly declares Regulatus.

That means the calendar does not only count time.

It carries propaganda.

To loyal citizens, AVD means civilization began after Viriatus's noble sacrifice.

To forbidden historians, AVD means the world began counting from a lie.

Same date.

Different perception.

Different meaning.

## What I Am Testing Here

This case study is where I started testing how one story detail can grow into a world system.

A family wound becomes public memory.

Public memory becomes law, ceremony, identity, and obedience.

Those systems later create pressure for NPCs, factions, quests, and conflicts.

I am not trying to build endless lore for its own sake.

I am testing whether old wounds can become modern pressure.
