# Quest Test: The Missing Medicine Logs

This case study tests whether the worldbuilding method can produce a quest from the same chain used in the rest of the project:

> experience -> emotion -> reason -> behavior -> atmosphere -> quest logic

The goal is not to create a procedural quest generator. The goal is to show that a playable quest can grow from world pressure without breaking the authored structure.

---

## 1. World Pressure

Devaar controls medical treatment, identification, records, and access to medicine.

In this district, the clinic is officially charitable, but treatment depends on documentation, status, and institutional approval.

---

## 2. Experience

Patients are denied medicine because their records are incomplete, incorrect, or politically suspicious.

Families wait outside the clinic while staff avoid eye contact.

A nurse watches people suffer because the system treats records as more real than bodies.

This is the lived experience created by the pressure.

---

## 3. Emotion

Patients feel fear and humiliation.

The nurse feels guilt and panic.

Clinic guards feel suspicion.

Devaar officials feel certainty that order must be protected.

The same pressure creates different emotions depending on role, position, and layer.

---

## 4. Reason

Patients think: if the record says I do not exist, I will die.

The nurse thinks: lying is the only way to save some of them.

The guards think: false records are how rebels enter the system.

The official thinks: mercy without order destroys the city.

Reason is not objective truth. It is how each group justifies its response to emotion.

---

## 5. Behavior

Patients trade identity papers.

The nurse quietly changes treatment logs.

Guards search people more aggressively.

Officials order random audits.

Behavior comes from reason, not from random quest needs.

---

## 6. Atmosphere

The clinic feels quiet, tense, and watched.

People whisper instead of complain.

Papers matter more than wounds.

Everyone looks guilty, even when they are sick.

Atmosphere is created by repeated behavior.

---

## 7. Quest Logic

The player finds mismatched treatment logs.

A patient begs the player not to expose the nurse.

A Devaar auditor asks the player to identify who altered the records.

The nurse claims the false records are the only reason children are still alive.

The quest appears because the behavior and atmosphere have created needs, obstacles, choices, and consequences.

---

## 8. Player-Facing Choices

- Expose the nurse to the auditor.
- Protect the nurse and hide the altered records.
- Use the records to blackmail the clinic.
- Give the records to rebels.
- Destroy the records.

These are authored choices, not automatically generated choices.

---

## 9. Consequence Check

Consequences should update future pressure and local layers, but should not automatically rewrite the active quest structure.

### Expose the nurse

Devaar control is reinforced. Clinic access becomes stricter. District fear increases.

### Protect the nurse

Local trust weakens Devaar authority. More patients survive, but audits intensify.

### Blackmail the clinic

Corruption increases. The player gains access, but patients become leverage.

### Give records to rebels

Rebel medical access improves. Devaar surveillance increases.

### Destroy the records

Immediate evidence disappears. Some patients are protected, but future treatment becomes harder to prove.

---

## 10. Backward Trace Test

The quest should be traceable backward.

Why does the nurse hide records?

Because she believes lying saves patients.

Why does she believe that?

Because she feels guilt and panic.

Why does she feel that?

Because she experiences patients being denied medicine.

Why does that happen?

Because Devaar medical control creates pressure through records and status.

If the quest can be traced backward through the chain, the diagram works.

---

## Result

This test shows that the method can produce:

- NPC motivation
- NPC behavior
- local atmosphere
- quest conflict
- player choice
- consequence
- future pressure

without claiming to be a procedural simulation system.

The stable method remains:

> experience -> emotion -> reason -> behavior -> atmosphere -> quest logic
